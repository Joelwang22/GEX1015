Determinism
Determinism: roughly, what will happen now is fixed by what happened in the past. Given the (same) past, only one possibility exists as to what will happen now.
We'll focus on physical/causal determinism: "the state of the cosmos at any one time is determined by its state at any prior time, together with the laws of nature. On this view, the state of the universe at any point in the past--say, exactly 1 billion years ago--and the laws of nature together fix the state of the universe at every future time. If this view is correct, then given the past and the laws, absolutely everything that happens--every supernova, every mugging--is determined to occur just as it does." (p.598)

An argument against free will
P1. Either determinism is true, or determinism is false. P2. If determinism is true, we don't have free will/moral responsibility. P3. If determinism is false, we don't have free will/moral responsibility.
C. We don't have free will/moral responsibility.
Those who claim that P2 is true are called incompatibilists (because they think determinism and free wil/moral responsibility are incompatible) and those who think that P2 is false are called compatibilists (because they think determinism and free will/moral responsibility are compatible).
Incompatibilists think P2 is true (that is, determinism is incompatible with free will/moral responsibility) because they think having alternative possibilities is necessary for free will/moral responsibility. (= We need alternative possibilities to have free will/moral responsibility = If we don't have alternative possibilities, we don't have free will/moral responsibility). An argument against free will

Those who claim that P2 is true are called incompatibilists (because they think determinism and free wil/moral responsibility are incompatible) and those who think that P2 is false are called compatibilists (because they think determinism and free will/moral responsibility are compatible).
Incompatibilists think P2 is true (that is, determinism is incompatible with free will/moral responsibility) because they think having alternative possibilities is necessary for free will/moral responsibility.
(Most) Compatibilists, on the other hand, think that alternative possibilities are not necessary for free will/moral responsibility and, thus, even if determinism is true, we can still have free will/moral responsibility.

<Challenges posed by Determinism vs. Indeterminism>
· Determinism threatens free will and moral responsibility because there are no alternative possibilities (you could not have done otherwise). But, if determinism is true, there is still a sense in which you're what determines your choice because factors in the past determine your choice only via your desires, values, etc. (although you couldn't have had different desires, values, etc.)
· On the other hand, if determinism is false, there are alternative possibilities (you could have done otherwise). The problem, though, is that it seems you're not what determines your choice. The choice seems random and arbitrary and not within your control.
