An argument against free will
P1. Either determinism is true, or determinism is false. P2. If determinism is true, we don't have free will/moral responsibility. P3. If determinism is false, we don't have free will/moral responsibility.
C. We don't have free will/moral responsibility. Those who claim that P2 is true are called incompatibilists (because they think determinism and free wil/moral responsibility are incompatible) and those who think that P2 is false are called compatibilists (because they think determinism and free will/moral responsibility are compatible).
Incompatibilists think P2 is true (that is, determinism is incompatible with free will/moral responsibility) because they think having alternative possibilities is necessary for free will/moral responsibility.
(Most) Compatibilists, on the other hand, think that alternative possibilities are not necessary for free will/moral responsibility and, thus, even if determinism is true, we can still have free will/moral responsibility.

Can an AI be conscious?
Now, let's turn to the position that AIs, at least some AIs, can be conscious.
One way to defend this position is to find a sufficient condition for consciousness and to show that at least some AIs can satisfy it.
One proposed sufficient condition is having exactly the same functional organization (the abstract pattern of causal interaction between various parts of a system, and between these parts and external inputs and outputs) as a conscious system, such as the human brain.
Not only indistinguishable from a human in terms of inputs and outputs (like the Chinese room) but also the inner "program" is the same, although it is implemented in a different material (e.g., silicon).
Two questions: Can we build an AI that satisfies this condition--an isomorph of the human brain? Is the condition really sufficient for consciousness?

Imagine that you go through a surgery that gradually replaces your neurons with microchips, one by one. Each chip performs precisely the same local function as the neuron it replaces.
(We're also assuming that you are not anesthetized and that you can talk with the doctor during the procedure.)

an isomorph an isomorph

an isomorph

There are three hypotheses about what happens to consciousness during this surgery.
1. The final isomorph is conscious.
2. The final isomorph is not conscious. 2-1. Consciousness suddenly disappears at some point during the procedure. 2-2. Consciousness gradually fades during the procedure.
According to David Chalmers, who proposed this thought experiment, 1 is most plausible.
2-1 is implausible because you go from full consciousness to no consciousness when a single neuron was replaced. This would be a bizarre discontinuity, unlike any other that we find in nature.
2-2 is also implausible given that your brain maintains the same functional organization throughout the process. This means, for example, that if the doctor asks you if you're still fully conscious, you'll judge and say that you are, throughout the process. So, you're completely out of touch with your own consciousness: you think it's normal when in fact it is fading away. This also seems quite bizarre.
